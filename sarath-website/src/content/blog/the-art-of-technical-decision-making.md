Every engineering team faces a constant stream of decisions: which framework to adopt, how to structure a service, when to refactor versus ship. The best engineers don't always pick the "correct" answer—they pick the *right* answer for their context.

## Start with constraints, not solutions

Before debating technologies, clarify what you're optimizing for. Is it time-to-market? Developer experience? Operational simplicity? Cost at scale? Write these down. They become your north star when opinions diverge.

## The reversible vs. irreversible framework

Jeff Bezos popularized the idea of Type 1 and Type 2 decisions. In software, most decisions are reversible. Treat them that way—make them quickly, learn, and iterate. Reserve deep deliberation for choices that are expensive to undo: data models, public APIs, core abstractions.

## Document the "why"

The decision itself matters less than the reasoning behind it. A short Architecture Decision Record (ADR) captures context, options considered, and the rationale. Future you—and your teammates—will thank you when revisiting choices six months later.

## Embrace good enough

Perfect is the enemy of shipped. A decision made with 80% confidence and executed well beats a paralyzed team waiting for certainty. Bias toward action, measure outcomes, and adjust.

The craft of engineering leadership isn't about having all the answers. It's about creating clarity, building consensus, and moving forward with intention.
