The gap between what we build and what users need is often wider than we admit. Feature factories churn out output; product teams create outcomes. The difference lies in how deeply you understand the humans on the other side of the screen.

## Talk to users early and often

Analytics tell you *what* happened. Conversations tell you *why*. Schedule regular user interviews—even 30 minutes a month compounds into invaluable insight. Ask about their workflow, frustrations, and workarounds before showing any prototypes.

## Solve jobs, not features

Users don't want a dashboard. They want to know if their business is healthy by Monday morning. Frame problems as jobs-to-be-done: "When I ___, I want to ___, so I can ___."

## Reduce friction relentlessly

Every click, field, and loading state is a tax on attention. Audit your flows for unnecessary steps. The best features often come from subtraction—removing complexity rather than adding capability.

## Ship, measure, iterate

Launch small, learn fast. A beta with ten engaged users beats a polished release nobody uses. Instrument key actions, watch session replays, and let data inform the next iteration.

Great products aren't built in isolation. They're co-created with the people who use them every day.
